Why Double First?

The "Reality Tax" addresses a fundamental cognitive bias in software engineering: the tendency to underestimate the complexity of a task by focusing on the "happy path." By forcing an initial expansion of effort before snapping to the next planning bucket, we account for the empirical uncertainty inherent in software development.

Formal Rationale

Let F(n) be the selected value in the Fibonacci planning sequence {0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ...}. The "Double and Snap" heuristic suggests the following transformation:

Estimate = min { F(k) | F(k) ≥ 2·F(n) }

However, observing the growth properties of the Fibonacci sequence, for any value where n > 2, the sequence converges toward the Golden Ratio (φ ≈ 1.618). This creates a mathematical tension:

F(n+1) < 2 · F(n) < F(n+2)

Applying F(n+1) (the next bucket) yields an average increase of 61.8%. While technically less than a literal doubling, it represents the "Golden Ratio Tax." It provides a significant buffer that respects the increasing granularity of the Fibonacci scale.

In this implementation, we prioritize the Step-Up Transformation:

F(n) → F(n+1)

This ensures that even the smallest "gut feeling" is immediately moved to a higher risk category, forcing teams to acknowledge that no task is as simple as it first appears.
