The Epistemology of the Reality Tax

Estimation is not a prediction; it is an empirical hypothesis. The "Reality Tax" addresses the fundamental cognitive bias in software engineering where developers focus on the "Happy Path," ignoring the inherent noise and undecidability of computation.

Formal Rationale

In the Fibonacci sequence {0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ...}, the ratio of successive terms converges to the Golden Ratio (φ ≈ 1.618). When we attempt to "double" a value in this discrete set, the lack of intermediate buckets forces a choice between two distinct mathematical shifts:

F(n+1) < 2 · F(n) < F(n+2)
Metric Merciful (Fn+1) Aggressive (Fn+2)
Effective Tax +61.8% (φ) +161.8% (φ²)

By choosing between these two, you are selecting your Epistemological Confidence. One accounts for the process; the other accounts for the chaos.

1. Merciful Logic: The Golden Ratio Buffer

The Merciful shift F(n) → F(n+1) applies a consistent ~62% tax. In software development, this is the "Testing Tax." It assumes the logic is understood, but the verification of that logic—writing unit tests, exploratory testing, and CI/CD overhead—requires the next bucket of effort.

2. Aggressive Logic: The "Strict Double" Penalty

Because Fibonacci buckets get further apart as they grow, the Aggressive shift F(n) → F(n+2) applies a massive ~162% tax. This is the "Research" (or "Legacy") tax, because you developers in this case cannot reasonable estimate what they do not know yet (in fact, they do not even know what they do not know).
